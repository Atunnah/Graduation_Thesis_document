\section{Deep Learning Approaches for OCR}

Deep learning has fundamentally transformed the field of Optical Character Recognition, shifting the paradigm from handcrafted feature engineering to end-to-end trainable neural architectures capable of learning rich visual representations directly from data. Unlike their traditional counterparts, deep learning-based OCR systems can generalize across diverse scripts, fonts, and document conditions with significantly reduced reliance on domain-specific heuristics. This section surveys the major deep learning methodologies that have driven state-of-the-art OCR performance across multiple languages and document types, tracing the progression from early convolutional approaches to modern transformer-based systems.

The foundations of deep learning in OCR were established with the introduction of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) as feature extractors for text region detection. Early work demonstrated that CNNs could learn spatially hierarchical features from raw image pixels, outperforming handcrafted descriptors on standard benchmarks \cite{LeCun1998}. Subsequent research introduced fully convolutional architectures tailored specifically for text detection in natural scene images. EAST (Efficient and Accurate Scene Text Detector) proposed a direct regression pipeline that predicts word or line-level bounding boxes in a single forward pass, substantially reducing inference time while maintaining competitive accuracy \cite{Zhou2017}. CRAFT (Character Region Awareness for Text Detection) extended this line of work by explicitly modeling character-level affinity, enabling robust detection of arbitrarily shaped and curved text instances \cite{Baek2019}. DBNet further advanced the field by introducing a differentiable binarization module into the segmentation pipeline, allowing the detection threshold to be learned adaptively during training and yielding strong performance on curved and multi-oriented text \cite{Liao2019}.

Parallel to progress in detection, the recognition branch of OCR underwent its own transformation. The Connectionist Text Proposal Network combined CNNs with Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) through Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC) loss, enabling the direct transcription of text line images without requiring explicit character segmentation \cite{Shi2015}. This sequence-to-sequence formulation proved particularly effective for languages with complex ligatures and variable character widths. Attention-based encoder-decoder architectures subsequently improved upon CTC-based models by allowing the decoder to selectively focus on relevant image regions during character prediction, as demonstrated by ASTER, which incorporated a rectification network to handle perspective distortion prior to recognition \cite{Shi2019}. More recent work, including ABINet, introduced an autonomous, bidirectional language model operating in parallel with the visual decoder, explicitly leveraging linguistic context to correct visually ambiguous predictions \cite{Fang2021}.

The advent of Vision Transformers (ViTs) and large-scale pretraining has further elevated OCR capabilities. Transformer architectures, originally developed for natural language processing \cite{Vaswani2023}, were adapted for image recognition tasks through the introduction of patch-based self-attention mechanisms, enabling models to capture long-range spatial dependencies that CNNs inherently struggle to model. In the OCR domain, this culminated in models such as PARSeq, which frames scene text recognition as a permutation-based language modeling problem, training a single model to jointly optimize over all factorizations of the output character sequence \cite{Bautista2022}. This strategy confers strong contextual reasoning ability at inference time, allowing the model to iteratively refine predictions using both left-to-right and right-to-left linguistic cues. On the detection side, transformer-based object detectors such as DETR eliminated the need for hand-designed anchor boxes and non-maximum suppression by formulating detection as a set prediction problem solved via bipartite matching \cite{Carion2020}. RT-DETR extended this framework to real-time settings through a hybrid encoder combining multi-scale convolutional feature extraction with cross-scale attention, achieving inference speeds suitable for practical deployment without sacrificing detection accuracy \cite{Zhao2024}.

Collectively, these developments reflect a broader trend in the field: the gradual replacement of modular, manually engineered pipelines with unified, data-driven architectures that jointly optimize all stages of text detection and recognition. The continued scaling of training data, model capacity, and self-supervised pretraining strategies suggests that deep learning will remain the dominant paradigm in OCR research for the foreseeable future.

\subsection{CNN-based Text Detection}

Convolutional Neural Networks have played a central role in advancing text detection, enabling systems to localize text regions in images with far greater robustness and accuracy than traditional methods. By learning hierarchical visual features directly from training data, CNN-based detectors eliminated the need for handcrafted descriptors and rule-based segmentation strategies that characterized earlier approaches. This section reviews three landmark CNN-based architectures for text detection, namely EAST, CRAFT, and DBNet, examining their core design principles, respective contributions, and inherent limitations.

EAST marked a significant departure from multi-stage text detection pipelines by proposing a fully convolutional, single-shot architecture that directly predicts text regions at the pixel level \cite{Zhou2017}. Rather than relying on a separate region proposal stage, EAST employs a feature pyramid network to aggregate multi-scale convolutional features, which are then passed to two output branches: one predicting a score map indicating text presence and another regressing geometric attributes such as bounding box coordinates and rotation angles. This streamlined design enables real-time inference while achieving competitive accuracy on standard benchmarks including ICDAR 2015 \cite{Karatzas2015}. However, EAST operates primarily at the word or line level and struggles with arbitrarily shaped or curved text instances, as its geometric output representation is constrained to rotated rectangles. Furthermore, its performance degrades on densely packed or overlapping text regions where pixel-level score maps become ambiguous.

CRAFT addressed several of these limitations by shifting the detection granularity from the word level to the character level \cite{Baek2019}. Rather than predicting bounding boxes directly, CRAFT trains a fully convolutional network to produce two heat maps: a region score indicating the likelihood of individual character centers, and an affinity score capturing the spatial relationship between adjacent characters. Text instances of arbitrary shape are then recovered by grouping character regions according to their affinity scores through post-processing. This character-centric formulation enables CRAFT to handle curved, irregular, and multi-oriented text with considerably greater flexibility than word-level detectors. A notable challenge in training CRAFT is the absence of character-level annotations in most public datasets, which the authors address through a weakly supervised training scheme using pseudo ground truth generated from a pretrained detector. While effective, this indirect supervision introduces noise and can cause inconsistencies in character boundary localization, particularly for small or heavily occluded characters.

DBNet (Differentiable Binarization Network) introduced a conceptually distinct contribution to CNN-based text detection by embedding an adaptive binarization module directly into the segmentation pipeline \cite{Liao2019}. Traditional segmentation-based detectors apply a fixed threshold to convert predicted probability maps into binary masks, a process that is decoupled from network training and therefore suboptimal. DBNet instead formulates binarization as a differentiable operation parameterized by a learned threshold map, allowing the network to jointly optimize segmentation and binarization end-to-end. This design yields sharper text boundary predictions and improves the separation of closely spaced text instances. DBNet achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple benchmarks including Total-Text \cite{Ch2017} and CTW1500 \cite{Yuliang2017}, with strong results on curved and arbitrarily shaped text. Its primary limitation lies in the post-processing step, where connected component analysis is still required to recover individual text instances from the binary map, introducing sensitivity to hyperparameter choices such as minimum region area thresholds.

Taken together, EAST, CRAFT, and DBNet illustrate the evolution of CNN-based text detection from single-shot regression toward increasingly flexible, segmentation-driven approaches capable of handling the geometric diversity of real-world text. While these architectures represent substantial advances over traditional methods, they share a common constraint: their convolutional inductive bias limits the receptive field and makes it difficult to model long-range spatial dependencies across the image. This constraint motivated the subsequent adoption of transformer-based architectures, which are discussed in the following section.

\subsection{Transformer-based Text Detection and RT-DETR}

The limitations of convolutional architectures in modeling long-range spatial dependencies motivated the adaptation of transformer-based models to the text detection domain. Originally introduced for natural language processing \cite{Vaswani2023}, the self-attention mechanism at the core of transformer architectures enables each position in a feature map to attend to all other positions simultaneously, regardless of spatial distance. This global receptive field is particularly valuable for text detection, where contextual cues from distant image regions frequently inform whether a local area contains text. The introduction of DETR by Carion et al. \cite{Carion2020} demonstrated that object detection could be reformulated as a direct set prediction problem, dispensing entirely with hand-designed components such as anchor boxes, non-maximum suppression, and region proposal networks. DETR encodes image features using a CNN backbone, processes them through a transformer encoder-decoder, and predicts a fixed set of object queries that are matched to ground truth instances via bipartite matching during training. Although DETR achieved competitive accuracy on general object detection benchmarks, its training convergence was slow and its inference speed was insufficient for deployment in latency-sensitive applications. These shortcomings prompted a series of follow-up works aimed at improving both efficiency and accuracy within the DETR framework.

Deformable DETR \cite{Zhu2021} addressed convergence speed by replacing dense self-attention with deformable attention, where each query attends only to a small set of learned reference points in the feature map rather than all spatial locations. This modification reduced computational complexity from quadratic to linear with respect to the number of feature map elements, enabling the use of multi-scale features and substantially accelerating training. Subsequent variants including DAB-DETR \cite{Liu2022} and DN-DETR \cite{Li2022} further improved training stability through dynamic anchor box formulations and denoising training strategies respectively. Despite these advances, transformer-based detectors continued to lag behind CNN-based counterparts in inference speed, limiting their practical applicability in real-time OCR pipelines.

RT-DETR \cite{Zhao2024} resolved this throughput bottleneck through a carefully engineered hybrid architecture that combines the feature extraction efficiency of convolutional networks with the global modeling capacity of transformers. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:rtdetr_architecture}, the overall architecture consists of three principal components: a convolutional backbone, a hybrid encoder, and a transformer decoder with IoU-aware query selection. It employs a pretrained convolutional backbone, such as ResNet \cite{He2015} or its variants, to extract multi-scale feature maps from the input image. These feature maps capture low-level texture and edge information at high resolution alongside high-level semantic information at lower resolutions, providing a rich multi-scale representation that is passed to the hybrid encoder.

The hybrid encoder is the central innovation distinguishing RT-DETR from prior transformer-based detectors. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:rtdetr_hybrid_encoder}, it consists of two sub-modules: an Attention-based Intra-scale Feature Interaction (AIFI) module and a CNN-based Cross-scale Feature Fusion (CCFM) module \cite{Zhao2024}. AIFI applies transformer self-attention exclusively to the highest-level feature map, where spatial resolution is lowest and the computational cost of global attention is manageable. This design choice captures long-range semantic dependencies without incurring the prohibitive cost of applying full attention across all scales. CCFM then fuses features across scales using convolutional operations, efficiently propagating the enriched semantic context from the high-level map to lower-level, higher-resolution maps. By restricting transformer operations to a single scale and delegating cross-scale fusion to lightweight convolutions, the hybrid encoder achieves a favorable balance between representational power and inference efficiency.

A key challenge in DETR-style detectors is initializing object queries effectively. Poorly initialized queries slow convergence and reduce detection accuracy, particularly for small or densely packed objects. RT-DETR addresses this through an IoU-aware query selection mechanism that initializes decoder queries from the top-scoring encoder output features, ranked jointly by classification confidence and predicted Intersection over Union (IoU) with ground truth boxes \cite{Zhao2024}. This ensures that the decoder begins from semantically meaningful spatial locations rather than random or uniformly distributed anchors, substantially improving both convergence speed and final detection accuracy. Unlike prior approaches that ranked proposals by classification score alone, the joint IoU-aware ranking yields better-calibrated initial queries for objects with high localization quality but moderate classification confidence, a common scenario in text detection where character and word regions share similar visual appearances.

The combined effect of these innovative design choices positions RT-DETR as the first transformer-based detector to achieve real-time inference speeds competitive with leading CNN-based detectors such as YOLOv8 \cite{Jocher2023}, while surpassing them in accuracy on standard benchmarks including COCO \cite{Lin2015}. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:rtdetr_performance}, RT-DETR with a ResNet-50 backbone achieves 53.1 AP at 108 frames per second on a single T4 GPU, outperforming YOLOv8-l by 1.0 AP at comparable speed \cite{Zhao2024}.

These properties make RT-DETR a compelling backbone for Vietnamese text detection in this work. Its anchor-free formulation eliminates the need to manually design aspect ratio priors for Vietnamese text lines, which vary considerably across document types. Its hybrid encoder captures both local character-level features and global line-level context without prohibitive computational overhead. Furthermore, its strong out-of-the-box performance on localization tasks, combined with the flexibility to fine-tune on domain-specific data, makes it well-suited for adaptation to the Vietnamese document domain, as detailed in the methodology chapter.

\subsection{Sequence-to-Sequence Models for Text Recognition}

The recognition of text in images presents a fundamentally different challenge from text detection, requiring a system to not only localize individual characters but also transcribe them into coherent sequences. Early deep learning approaches to this problem drew heavily from the speech recognition community, where sequence modeling had already matured through the application of recurrent architectures and probabilistic alignment frameworks. This section traces the evolution of text recognition models from early CNN-RNN hybrids through attention-based approaches, establishing the progression that motivated the development of more powerful transformer-based recognizers.

The Connectionist Text Proposal Network, more commonly known as CRNN, established the foundational architecture for sequence-based text recognition by combining a convolutional feature extractor with a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network \cite{Shi2015}. The convolutional layers extract a sequence of local feature descriptors from the input text image, which are then fed column-by-column into the recurrent network to produce a sequential encoding of the entire text line. The output sequence is decoded using CTC loss, which marginalizes over all possible alignments between the predicted label sequence and the ground truth transcription, eliminating the need for explicit character-level segmentation during both training and inference \cite{Graves2006}. CRNN demonstrated strong performance on printed and scene text benchmarks and became widely adopted as a baseline architecture due to its simplicity and computational efficiency. However, its recurrent bottleneck limited parallelization during training, and the CTC decoding assumption of conditional independence between output labels prevented the model from explicitly leveraging linguistic context across character positions.

To address the sensitivity of recognition systems to geometric distortions introduced by perspective, curvature, and irregular text layouts, ASTER incorporated a learnable spatial transformer network as a rectification frontend prior to the recognition backbone \cite{Shi2019}. The rectification module predicts a thin-plate spline transformation that warps the input image into a canonical, approximately horizontal text layout before passing it to an attention-based sequence decoder. This two-stage design, combining geometric correction with attentive decoding, substantially improved recognition accuracy on curved and perspective-distorted text benchmarks such as SVT-Perspective \cite{Phan2013}. Despite these gains, the rectification network introduced additional parameters and inference overhead, and its performance remained dependent on the quality of the predicted transformation, which could degrade on severely distorted or low-resolution inputs.

Attention-based encoder-decoder architectures extended the sequence recognition paradigm by enabling the decoder to selectively attend to spatially relevant regions of the encoded feature map at each decoding step, rather than compressing the entire input into a fixed-length context vector \cite{Bahdanau2016}. This soft alignment mechanism allowed recognition models to handle variable-length inputs more gracefully and improved their ability to resolve locally ambiguous characters by incorporating broader contextual information. AON further explored the role of character ordering in attention-based recognition, demonstrating that modeling the four cardinal reading directions and arbitrating between them improved robustness to irregular text orientations \cite{Cheng2018}. SAR (Show, Attend and Read) combined a two-dimensional attention mechanism with a convolutional encoder and LSTM decoder, enabling the model to attend directly to two-dimensional feature maps rather than collapsed one-dimensional sequences, which proved particularly beneficial for characters with vertically complex structures \cite{Li2019}.

A significant conceptual advance came with ABINet, which decoupled visual and linguistic modeling into two independent branches operating in parallel \cite{Fang2021}. The visual model produces an initial character sequence prediction from image features alone, while a separately trained bidirectional language model refines this prediction by injecting linguistic context derived from the surrounding character sequence. This iterative refinement strategy enables the language model to correct visually ambiguous predictions by leveraging global sequence-level constraints, without requiring the language model to be differentiably integrated into the visual encoder. ABINet achieved state-of-the-art results on multiple scene text benchmarks and demonstrated that explicit linguistic modeling, rather than implicit context captured by recurrent decoders, was a critical ingredient for high-accuracy recognition. However, its reliance on a separately pretrained language model introduced additional complexity and made end-to-end training more involved.

Taken together, the progression from CRNN to ABINet reflects a consistent trend toward richer contextual modeling in text recognition, moving from locally conditioned CTC decoding through soft attention to explicit bidirectional language modeling. Each step addressed a specific limitation of its predecessors while introducing new design trade-offs between model complexity, training stability, and inference efficiency. These accumulated insights laid the groundwork for the fully transformer-based recognition architectures discussed in the following section.

\subsection{PARSeq and Permutation-based Recognition}
\label{sec:parseq_and_permutation_based_recognition}

The limitations of prior sequence recognition architectures, particularly their reliance on fixed decoding orders and separately trained language models, motivated the development of a more unified framework capable of jointly optimizing visual and linguistic modeling within a single end-to-end trainable system. PARSeq addresses these shortcomings through a principled reformulation of scene text recognition as a permutation language modeling problem, drawing on advances in vision transformers and masked language modeling to produce a recognizer that is simultaneously flexible, contextually rich, and computationally efficient \cite{Bautista2022}.

As shown in Figure \ref{fig:parseq_architecture}, the architectural foundation of PARSeq is a Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder \cite{Dosovitskiy2021}, which processes the input text image by dividing it into a fixed grid of non-overlapping patches and projecting each patch into a high-dimensional embedding space. These patch embeddings, augmented with learnable positional encodings, are passed through a stack of transformer self-attention layers that model pairwise dependencies across all spatial positions simultaneously. Unlike CNN-based encoders that aggregate spatial context through successive local convolutions, the ViT encoder captures global image structure from the first layer onward, enabling the model to relate distant character regions without requiring deep stacking of convolutional layers. The resulting sequence of patch representations serves as the visual context from which character predictions are subsequently decoded.

    \caption[Overview of PARSeq]{\textit{Overview of PARSeq \cite{Bautista2022}}} 
    \label{fig:parseq_architecture}
\end{figure}

\vspace{3cm}

The central innovation of PARSeq lies in its permutation-based training strategy, inspired by the XLNet language model \cite{Yang2020}. Rather than training the decoder to predict characters exclusively in a fixed left-to-right order, PARSeq samples multiple permutations of the output character sequence during training and optimizes the model to correctly predict each character given all other characters that precede it in the sampled permutation order. This approach trains a single shared decoder to internalize all possible factorizations of the joint sequence probability, implicitly learning a bidirectional language model without requiring a separately pretrained component. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:parseq_permutation}, at inference time this confers the ability to iteratively refine predictions: an initial left-to-right decoding pass produces a candidate sequence, which is then used as context for a subsequent refinement pass that can correct individual characters using both preceding and following character context. This refinement mechanism allows PARSeq to resolve visually ambiguous characters that unidirectional decoders would misclassify, particularly in languages with dense diacritical marks such as Vietnamese.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{images/parseq_permutation.png}
    \caption[Permutation-based training strategy of PARSeq]{\textit{Permutation-based training strategy of PARSeq \cite{Bautista2022}}} 
    \label{fig:parseq_permutation}
\end{figure}

A further practical advantage of PARSeq is its support for both autoregressive and non-autoregressive decoding within the same model, controlled entirely through the attention mask supplied to the decoder \cite{Bautista2022}. In autoregressive mode, each character is predicted conditioned on all previously decoded characters, yielding higher accuracy at the cost of sequential decoding steps. In non-autoregressive mode, all characters are predicted simultaneously from visual features alone, enabling parallel decoding at the cost of reduced contextual reasoning. This flexibility allows practitioners to trade off accuracy against inference speed depending on deployment constraints, without retraining the model.

PARSeq was evaluated on a comprehensive suite of standard scene text recognition benchmarks, including IIIT5K \cite{Mishra2012}, SVT \cite{Wang2011}, ICDAR 2013 \cite{Karatzas2013}, ICDAR 2015 \cite{Karatzas2015}, SVTP \cite{Phan2013}, and CUTE80 \cite{Risnumawan2014}. Across these benchmarks, PARSeq achieved state-of-the-art accuracy while maintaining a substantially smaller parameter count than competing methods such as ABINet \cite{Fang2021} and SRN \cite{Yu2020}, demonstrating that permutation-based training is a more parameter-efficient mechanism for injecting linguistic context than maintaining a separate language model branch. On the Union14M-Benchmark \cite{Jiang2023}, a more recent and challenging evaluation suite covering artistic, multi-oriented, and occluded text, PARSeq similarly demonstrated strong generalization across diverse visual conditions.

The properties of PARSeq make it a compelling choice as the recognition stage of the Vietnamese OCR system developed in this work. Its ViT encoder captures the fine-grained spatial detail necessary to distinguish Vietnamese diacritical marks that differ only by subtle stroke variations, while its permutation-trained decoder provides the bidirectional linguistic context needed to resolve ambiguities arising from characters that are visually similar under degraded image conditions. Furthermore, its compact architecture and support for vocabulary extension through straightforward modification of the output projection layer facilitate adaptation to the full Vietnamese character set, as detailed in the methodology chapter.

\subsection{Oriented Text Detection and Rotation-Aware Loss Functions}
\label{sec:oriented_text_detection_and_rotation_aware_loss_functions_2_2_5}

Standard object detection frameworks, including the axis-aligned architectures surveyed in preceding sections, represent detected regions as horizontal bounding boxes parameterized by their center coordinates, width, and height. While this representation is adequate for documents with consistently horizontal text layouts, it fails to tightly enclose text instances that appear at arbitrary orientations, a common condition in real-world document images where skewed scanning, physical page curvature, or diverse document types introduce non-horizontal text lines. For such cases, axis-aligned boxes either over-expand to encompass rotated text, introducing substantial background noise into the cropped region passed to the recognizer, or under-fit the text extent entirely, causing character truncation at box boundaries. These failure modes motivate the adoption of oriented bounding box representations, in which each detection is additionally parameterized by a rotation angle, enabling tight enclosure of text instances regardless of their orientation.

\subsubsection{Oriented Bounding Box Detection for Scene Text}
The problem of detecting arbitrarily oriented text has been an active research direction since the limitations of axis-aligned detectors became apparent on real-world scene text benchmarks. Early work on oriented text detection adapted region proposal networks to generate rotated candidate regions by augmenting the anchor parameterization with a discrete set of orientation bins. RRPN (Rotation Region Proposal Networks) \cite{Ma2018} extended the Faster RCNN framework by introducing rotation-aware anchors spanning multiple orientations and an RoI rotation pooling operation that extracts features from arbitrarily rotated candidate regions. This enabled the detector to localize text at angles not representable by axis-aligned anchors, achieving substantially improved recall on benchmarks containing multi-oriented text such as ICDAR 2015 \cite{Karatzas2015}. However, RRPN's reliance on a discrete set of predefined rotation anchors limited its angular resolution and required careful anchor design to cover the expected range of text orientations in the target domain.

TextBoxes++ \cite{Liao2018} approached the problem from a single-shot detection perspective, extending the SSD framework with irregular convolutional filters and output layers that directly regress rotated quadrilateral bounding boxes rather than axis-aligned rectangles. By predicting four corner offsets relative to a default box anchor, TextBoxes++ could represent not only rotated rectangles but also general quadrilaterals, enabling accurate enclosure of perspective-distorted text instances. This direct regression formulation avoided the angular discretization problem of anchor-based approaches but introduced training instability arising from the discontinuous nature of angular regression targets near boundary angles.

More recent work has integrated oriented detection capabilities into transformer-based frameworks, which are better suited to the anchor-free, set-prediction paradigm adopted in this thesis. Oriented RCNN \cite{Xie2021} demonstrated that a simple modification to the region proposal head, replacing axis-aligned box regression with five-parameter oriented box regression, sufficed to achieve state-of-the-art oriented detection performance without requiring specialized rotation-aware pooling operations. In the context of this work, RT-DETR is extended with an additional angle prediction head that regresses orientation as a two-dimensional unit vector in sine-cosine space, rather than as a scalar angle in degrees. This sine-cosine encoding avoids the angular discontinuity problem that arises when scalar angle regression crosses the boundary between 0 and 360 degrees, ensuring smooth and continuous gradient flow during training regardless of the predicted orientation.

\subsubsection{Gaussian Wasserstein Distance Loss for Oriented Box Regression}
A fundamental challenge in training oriented object detectors is the design of a regression loss that accurately measures the geometric discrepancy between predicted and ground truth oriented bounding boxes. Standard IoU-based losses, which compute the ratio of intersection area to union area between two boxes, are well-defined and geometrically meaningful for axis-aligned boxes but become computationally expensive and numerically unstable for rotated boxes, particularly when the predicted and ground truth boxes have significantly different orientations or when one box is nearly degenerate \cite{Yang2022}. Moreover, IoU is identically zero for any pair of non-overlapping boxes regardless of their spatial proximity, providing no gradient signal to guide the detector toward correct predictions when initial estimates are far from the ground truth.

Yang et al. \cite{Yang2022} proposed the Gaussian Wasserstein Distance (GWD) loss as a principled alternative to IoU-based losses for oriented bounding box regression. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:gwd_gaussian}, the key insight is that an oriented bounding box parameterized by its center coordinates, width, height, and rotation angle can be equivalently represented as a two-dimensional Gaussian distribution, where the mean vector encodes the box center and the covariance matrix encodes the box shape and orientation through its eigenvalue decomposition. Formally, given an oriented box with center $(\mu_x, \mu_y)$, half-axes $(w/2, h/2)$, and rotation matrix $R$, the corresponding Gaussian has mean $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_x, \mu_y)$ and covariance:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = R \begin{pmatrix} (w/2)^2 & 0 \\ 0 & (h/2)^2 \end{pmatrix} R^\top
\end{equation}

The geometric discrepancy between two oriented boxes is then measured as the squared Wasserstein-2 distance between their corresponding Gaussian distributions:
\begin{equation}
\text{GWD}^2(\mathcal{G}_1, \mathcal{G}_2) = |\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2|^2 + \text{Tr}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_1 + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_2 - 2\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_1^{1/2} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_2 \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_1^{1/2}\right)^{1/2}\right)
\end{equation}
The final GWD loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{GWD}} = 1 - \frac{1}{1 + \sqrt{\text{GWD}^2} / \tau}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a scaling hyperparameter. This formulation yields a loss value in $[0, 1]$ that is differentiable with respect to all box parameters including the rotation angle, provides meaningful gradient signal even for non-overlapping boxes, and degrades gracefully under near-degenerate box configurations where IoU-based losses become numerically unstable \cite{Yang2022}.

    equivalence between an oriented bounding box $B(x, y, w, h, \theta)$ and

Compared to the commonly used smooth-L1 loss applied independently to each box parameter, GWD loss treats the oriented box as a unified geometric object and penalizes deviations in all parameters jointly through a single geometrically grounded metric. This is particularly important for text line detection in Vietnamese documents, where text lines frequently appear at slight skew angles due to scanning imperfections, and where the regression targets for width, height, and angle are strongly correlated. The adoption of GWD loss in this work, alongside a sine-cosine angle encoding and an angle-specific regression head in the extended RT-DETR architecture, constitutes one of the principal technical contributions of the methodology chapter.
