\section{Traditional Optical Character Recognition Methods}

Traditional Optical Character Recognition (OCR) methods, developed primarily before the advent of modern machine learning and deep learning techniques, rely heavily on rule-based, template-based, or heuristic-driven approaches to transform text from images into machine-readable formats. These methods leverage image processing techniques, handcrafted feature extraction, and predefined geometric patterns to recognize characters, often with a focus on digitized scanned documents. While foundational to the field of automated text recognition, these approaches are characterized by their dependence on manual effort and domain-specific expertise, which limits their scalability and adaptability in complex visual environments. This section provides an in-depth review of key paradigms in traditional OCR, traces the evolution of these methods over time, and highlights their contributions and shortcomings.

One of the earliest notable efforts in traditional OCR is the work of Mori, Suen, and Yamamoto \cite{Mori1992}, who synthesized and developed first-generation OCR techniques. The approach during this period utilized basic image processing techniques to perform "template matching," comparing images pixel-by-pixel against a predefined library of templates. While this method was straightforward and computationally efficient for uniform, typewritten documents, the generated results were highly sensitive to variations, making the system brittle when faced with different font styles or sizes.

Building on this foundation, the OCR field advanced further with research on structural and topological feature extraction, exemplified by the work of Tappert et al. \cite{Tappert1990}. This system employed more complex techniques to extract key geometric characteristics from text,such as loops, lines, and intersections-to serve as features for classification algorithms. This approach was particularly valuable in processing standardized printed documents. However, it required significant manual tuning to design noise-robust features, highlighting a key limitation of traditional methods: their heavy dependence on human intervention.

Another landmark contribution came from the application of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) to text recognition, widely utilized in works such as those by Elms \cite{Elms1996}. This method begins by extracting features from vertical slices of a text line (sliding window) to create a sequence of observations. This approach was groundbreaking because it moved beyond rigid, isolated character segmentation, enabling the recognition of cursive scripts or noisy text. However, its effectiveness depended heavily on the quality of the manually crafted sliding-window features and the accompanying statistical language model.

Researchers further expanded the scope of OCR by focusing on highly complex languages, particularly Vietnamese \cite{Le2025}. Traditional studies on Vietnamese text often emphasized addressing complex diacritics by combining rule-based projection profiles to separate main characters from tonal marks, followed by heuristic scoring methods to reassociate them. However, this method struggled to scale across various types of background noise or scene text images in natural environments.

Beyond these foundational works, several other studies have enriched the landscape of traditional OCR methods. Lenet and colleagues pioneered the application of convolutional neural network precursors combined with traditional sliding-window classifiers for digit recognition \cite{LeCun1998}. This hybrid approach improved upon purely template-based systems by incorporating learned local features, but still required carefully engineered preprocessing pipelines and struggled to generalize beyond constrained digit vocabularies. Casey and Lecolinet \cite{Casey1996} took a different tack with their taxonomy of character recognition strategies tailored for printed documents. Their survey systematized approaches using structural decomposition, in which characters are broken into primitive strokes and reassembled via graph-matching algorithms. This approach excelled in domain-specific contexts such as postal code recognition, but its reliance on a fixed set of structural primitives restricted its flexibility when confronted with diverse font families. Adapting the system to new scripts necessitated redesigning the primitive library, a labor-intensive process. Another relevant contribution is from Nagy \cite{Nagy2000}, who explored document layout analysis as a precursor to character recognition. Their system uses geometric segmentation to identify text blocks, columns, and lines before passing regions to a character classifier, marking a step forward in handling multi-column documents. However, its dependence on clean binarization and rigid layout assumptions limited its robustness across varied real-world document conditions.
Traditional OCR methods have notable strengths. Their rule-based and template-driven design ensures predictable, interpretable outputs, ideal for controlled settings such as digitizing typewritten administrative records or standardized printed forms. They perform reliably on uniform, high-resolution documents with consistent fonts and layouts. Unlike data-driven approaches, these methods require minimal labeled datasets and computational resources, making them suitable for early digitization efforts or resource-limited environments.
However, their drawbacks are significant. Handcrafted features and rigid templates limit scalability and adaptability across diverse document types. For instance, projection-profile methods for Vietnamese diacritics require domain-specific tuning, hindering generalization to natural scene text or degraded historical documents. HMM-based systems similarly depend heavily on manually crafted sliding-window features, making them brittle under varying noise conditions. Additionally, these methods often lack the capacity to model contextual dependencies across characters or words, producing recognition outputs that are locally plausible but globally inconsistent, particularly for morphologically complex scripts such as Vietnamese. This has driven the shift toward automated, data-driven techniques.
In summary, traditional OCR methods laid critical groundwork, offering practical tools and foundational insights for automated text recognition. They established key paradigms, template matching, structural feature extraction, and statistical sequence modeling, that continue to inform modern OCR research. Over time, the field progressed from basic pixel-level comparisons to more sophisticated geometric and probabilistic methods, reflecting the broader evolution of pattern recognition as a discipline.
Still, the labor-intensive nature of these methods and their struggles with complex scripts, diverse layouts, and noisy real-world images have fueled the adoption of modern approaches. Machine learning and deep learning, leveraging large annotated datasets and neural architectures, enable text recognition systems with far greater flexibility and robustness. These advancements, explored in subsequent sections of this thesis, build on the legacy of traditional OCR while fundamentally overcoming its core limitations.
