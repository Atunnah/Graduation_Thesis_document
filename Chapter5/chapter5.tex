This thesis presented the design, implementation, and evaluation of a cascaded deep learning architecture for Vietnamese OCR, combining a fine-tuned RT-DETR V2 oriented text line detector with a fine-tuned PARSeq permutation-based sequence recognizer. The system was developed to address two gaps identified in Chapter \ref{chapter:related_works}: the absence of a Vietnamese OCR pipeline capable of handling arbitrarily oriented text lines, and the limited performance of existing recognizers on the morphologically complex Vietnamese character set.

\section{Conclusions}

The thesis makes four principal technical contributions. First, RT-DETR V2 was extended with an auxiliary oriented bounding box detection capability via a per-layer angle regression head with sine-cosine output parameterization and GWD loss \cite{Yang2022}, enabling orientation prediction across the full $[0^\circ, 360^\circ)$ range without angular discontinuity. Second, a large-scale synthetic data generation pipeline was developed, producing approximately 130,000 annotated detection images across seven document layout archetypes, with a key correction to the angle distribution collapse caused by \texttt{cv2.minAreaRect} to ensure uniform angular coverage. Third, PARSeq was adapted to Vietnamese through vocabulary extension to approximately 310 composite character tokens, class-weighted cross-entropy training, and a phonological validity mask at inference time, fine-tuned on a 2,000,000-image synthetic recognition corpus. Fourth, a modular pipeline architecture was implemented connecting the two stages through perspective-corrected cropping, converting oriented text line regions to upright crops before recognition.

The experimental evaluation confirms the effectiveness of the proposed system on both pipeline stages. On detection, the fine-tuned RT-DETR achieves F1@0.50 of 0.501 and mAP@0.5:0.95 of 0.147, compared to 0.134 and 0.008 for the EAST baseline. The advantage is especially pronounced at stricter IoU thresholds (F1@0.75: 0.188 vs. 0.021), confirming that oriented detection and GWD loss contribute directly to localization tightness. On recognition, the fine-tuned PARSeq achieves a CER of 10.18\% and WER of 34.71\%, outperforming all evaluated baselines including VietOCR by 1.16 and 2.73 percentage points respectively.

The remaining WER of 34.71\% reflects that Vietnamese OCR remains challenging. Primary bottlenecks are visual ambiguity between diacritically similar characters under image degradation and the domain gap between the synthetic training distribution and real-world documents — both addressable through future data collection and joint stage optimization.

The system's practical value lies in its modular, end-to-end deployable design suited to Vietnamese administrative document digitization, where the \texttt{LineDetector} and \texttt{TextRecognizer} components can be independently upgraded as improved checkpoints become available.

\section{Future Works}

Several directions for improvement follow from the identified limitations.

\textbf{Real data augmentation.} The most impactful near-term improvement would be collecting and annotating a modest set of real Vietnamese document images for fine-tuning, as even small real-world datasets have been shown to close a large portion of the synthetic-to-real domain gap.

\textbf{Improved angle regression.} The angle head could be extended to predict an orientation distribution rather than a point estimate, enabling uncertainty-aware NMS and providing the recognition stage with localization reliability signals. A polygon-based bounding representation could additionally accommodate curved or perspective-distorted text lines.

\textbf{Joint end-to-end fine-tuning.} The cascaded design prevents gradient flow from the recognition stage back to the detector. Incorporating differentiable cropping operations, as explored in end-to-end two-stage pipelines, would allow the detector to optimize bounding box parameters specifically for the recognizer's sensitivity to diacritical placement.

\textbf{Handwritten and historical text.} Extension to handwritten Vietnamese and to historical documents containing archaic typefaces or Chu Nom characters would broaden the system's applicability and is of significant archival value.

\textbf{Multi-language extension.} The framework is language-agnostic in architecture; extending it to other Southeast Asian scripts with complex diacritics (Thai, Lao, Khmer) requires only language-specific training corpora without structural changes.
